What is Quantum Computing?

Quantum computing is a revolutionary computing paradigm that leverages the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in fundamentally different ways than classical computers.

Key Differences

  • Uses quantum mechanics principles
  • Qubits can be in multiple states simultaneously
  • Exponentially more powerful for certain problems
  • Still in early development stage

Quantum Superposition

Superposition is the ability of quantum systems to exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured. Think of a coin spinning in the air - until it lands, it's neither heads nor tails but both.

Key Concepts

  • Qubits can be 0, 1, or both simultaneously
  • Measurement collapses the superposition
  • Enables quantum parallelism
  • Foundation of quantum computing power

Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement occurs when particles become correlated in such a way that measuring one instantly affects its partner, regardless of distance. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance".

Key Properties

  • Particles become mysteriously connected
  • Measurement of one affects the other instantly
  • No classical analog exists
  • Essential for quantum algorithms

Quantum Gates

Quantum gates are the building blocks of quantum circuits. They perform operations on qubits to manipulate their quantum states, creating and manipulating superposition and entanglement.

Basic Gates

X Gate (NOT): Flips the qubit state
H Gate (Hadamard): Creates superposition
CNOT Gate: Creates entanglement between qubits
